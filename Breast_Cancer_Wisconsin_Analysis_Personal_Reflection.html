Introduction

Breast cancer remains one of the leading causes of cancer-related morbidity and mortality worldwide. Its early detection is critical for improving patient outcomes, guiding surgical decisions, and tailoring adjuvant therapy. As a future surgical oncologist, understanding tumor characteristics from both a clinical and data-driven perspective is invaluable.

In this analysis, I explore the Breast Cancer Wisconsin (Diagnostic) Dataset [1], focusing on identifying which tumor cell features are most strongly associated with malignancy and building a simple predictive model.
While this is a beginner-friendly data science project, it mirrors the type of analytical thinking needed in surgical oncology research.

Dataset Source: Kaggle - Breast Cancer Wisconsin Data

Conclusion

From this beginner-friendly analysis, a clear pattern emerges. The majority of patientsover 350received a benign diagnosis, meaning their tumors were non-cancerous. In contrast, just over 210 patients were diagnosed with malignant tumors, representing cancerous growths that have the potential to spread and become life-threatening. This distribution carries two key messages. Clinically, it offers hope: most cases in this dataset are benign, highlighting that not all breast lumps lead to cancer. However, from a public health perspective, the presence of more than 200 malignant cases is a stark reminder that breast cancer remains a significant concern. Each malignant case represents an urgent need for timely detection, diagnosis, and treatment. From a data science standpoint, this imbalance in the datasetwhere benign cases outnumber malignant onesmust be addressed carefully. Predictive models trained on this data could become biased toward predicting benign outcomes unless techniques like resampling or class weighting are applied. Balancing model sensitivity to both classes is essential to avoid missed malignant diagnoses, which could have serious consequences for patient outcomes. In short, this tells a story of guarded optimism: while benign diagnoses are more common, the malignant cases demand our full attentionboth in clinical care and in the algorithms we design to support it.
Also, several tumor characteristics stood out as strong predictors of malignancy notably radius_mean, texture_mean, and concave_points_mean. In clinical practice, these features correspond to the microscopic and macroscopic appearance of tumor cells, which influence staging, prognosis, and the extent of surgical resection.

While this is only a foundational project, it mirrors how data science can enhance surgical oncology decision-making. In the future, such models could be expanded with imaging data, genomic profiles, and patient histories to create more robust predictive tools.
